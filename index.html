Governance
Pattern Language

An open library of observed patterns in network governance, gathered over 8 years of work across philanthropic and social change ecosystems. Each pattern names something real — something that blocks or enables how groups make decisions, distribute power, and adapt over time.

Block A pattern that creates problems — named so it can be recognised
Unblock A pattern that opens possibilities — a move worth making

Theme 01

Authority & Coordination Dynamics

Blocks — patterns that concentrate and calcify power
⚡
The Bottleneck Leader
Block
Centralizing leadership to manage decision overload leads to dependency.
Problem
In rapidly evolving environments, decision-making becomes overwhelming. Individuals feel lost without clear direction.
How it plays out
Power concentrates in a single leader who "keeps things organized" — which creates stagnation, dependency, and risks burnout or authoritarianism.
Related patterns
Shared StewardshipConsent, Not ConsensusPower as ResourceRight Decision, Right LevelNaming the Invisible
🚪
The Gatekeeper Effect
Block
Filtering access in growing organizations concentrates control.
Problem
As organizations expand, filtering access to key resources becomes challenging — a few individuals become de facto gatekeepers.
How it plays out
The attempt to maintain quality ends up restricting participation and innovation by concentrating control.
Related patterns
Shared StewardshipMaking Power VisibleParticipation with Purpose
👑
The Cult of the Founder
Block
Charismatic leadership creates dependency and stifles succession.
Problem
When groups form around a charismatic leader, power becomes overly concentrated, stifling governance evolution and suppressing succession.
How it plays out
Dependency on a single figure limits fresh ideas and undermines distributed leadership.
Related patterns
Shared StewardshipRight Decision, Right LevelNaming the Invisible
Unblocks — moves that distribute and revitalise authority
🤝
Shared Stewardship
Unblock
Rotates leadership roles to distribute power and enhance inclusivity.
Problem it addresses
Centralized leadership leads to burnout and exclusion by confining decision-making to a small group.
The move
Rotate leadership roles and share responsibility across the community to distribute power, enhance resilience, and encourage inclusivity.
Related patterns
The Bottleneck LeaderThe Cult of the FounderConsent, Not ConsensusPower as Resource
✓
Consent, Not Consensus
Unblock
Uses consent-based decision-making to balance inclusion with action.
Problem it addresses
Striving for full consensus can slow decisions and marginalize minority viewpoints, leading to ineffective, watered-down outcomes.
The move
Adopt a consent-based approach — "good enough for now, safe enough to try" — to balance inclusivity with timely action.
Related patterns
Shared StewardshipRight Decision, Right LevelNaming the Invisible
⚙
Power as Resource, Not Authority
Unblock
Reframes power as enabling collective action rather than control.
Problem it addresses
Viewing power solely as control creates hierarchical bottlenecks and undermines collaborative efforts.
The move
Reframe power as a resource that supports collective action, care, and innovation rather than serving as a tool for domination.
Related patterns
Shared StewardshipConsent, Not ConsensusRight Decision, Right Level
🎯
Right Decision, Right Level
Unblock
Aligns decision-making authority with the group best suited to act.
Problem it addresses
Decision-making at inappropriate levels creates bottlenecks or concentrates power inefficiently.
The move
Align decision-making authority with the group or domain most equipped to handle it, ensuring responsiveness and contextual insight.
Related patterns
Shared StewardshipConsent, Not ConsensusPower as Resource
👁
Naming the Invisible
Unblock
Makes implicit dynamics explicit to clarify roles and accountability.
Problem it addresses
Hidden assumptions and unspoken agreements lead to confusion and misaligned expectations.
The move
Use visual tools and open dialogue to reveal and clarify invisible structures, roles, and accountability.
Related patterns
Shared StewardshipConsent, Not ConsensusRight Decision, Right Level

Handling Complexity

Blocks — patterns that oversimplify and over-plan
🌀

Block
Oversimplification masks the true complexity of challenges.
Problem
Decision-makers oversimplify issues, masking the intricate nature of systemic challenges.
How it plays out
Simplistic narratives lead to ill-prepared strategies that fail to address real-world intricacies.
Related patterns
Sense-Making FirstHold Multiple Truths
📊
The Predictive Fallacy
Block
Rigid long-term plans assume a predictable future, reducing flexibility.
Problem
Relying on fixed, long-term plans under the assumption of predictability causes inflexibility when circumstances change.
How it plays out
Over-planning and the need for adaptable, responsive governance practices are in direct tension.

Problem

📦
The Black Box Decision
Block
Opaque decision-making erodes trust and excludes affected stakeholders.
Problem
When decisions are made behind closed doors, trust is eroded and those affected feel excluded.
How it plays out
A drive for efficiency at the expense of openness undermines accountability and inclusiveness.

Participation as Performance
Block
Superficial participation creates an illusion of inclusion.
Problem
Superficial participatory processes give an appearance of inclusion while key decisions remain in the hands of a few.
How it plays out
Performative participation leads to disengagement and cynicism when genuine influence is lacking.
Related patterns

The Token Inclusion Trap
Block
Symbolic inclusion without real power-sharing undermines engagement.
Problem

How it plays out
True inclusion requires transferring substantive decision-making authority rather than mere symbolism.

Theme 05

Boundaries & Inclusion

Blocks — patterns that close off and exclude
🧱
Impenetrable Boundaries
Block
Rigid boundaries inhibit new participation and foster insularity.
Problem
Overly rigid boundaries inhibit new participation and foster insularity, limiting growth and innovation.
How it plays out
Overprotecting group identity at the cost of openness leads to stagnation and groupthink.
Related patterns
Permeable BoundariesInvite, Don't ObligeHolding the Edge
🎲
Entry by Favoritism
Block
Favoritism in entry processes undermines transparency and diversity.
Problem
Selecting new members based on personal relationships or favoritism undermines transparency and curtails diversity.
How it plays out
Informal, exclusionary practices call for merit- and relationship-based entry processes instead.
Related patterns
Entry by RelationshipParticipation with Purpose
🔒
Closed Networks
Block
Exclusionary networks risk echo chambers and limit innovation.
Problem
Insular networks that exclude external voices risk becoming echo chambers, reinforcing biases and limiting innovation.
How it plays out
Overly exclusive networks fail to adapt to external insights and become brittle over time.
Related patterns
Permeable BoundariesInvite, Don't ObligeHolding the Edge

🌊
Permeable Boundaries
Unblock
Creates flexible boundaries that welcome new ideas while preserving core values.
Problem it addresses
Overly rigid boundaries hinder growth by preventing fresh perspectives from entering the group.
The move
Establish flexible boundaries that allow new ideas to enter while preserving essential values and focus.
Related patterns
Impenetrable BoundariesClosed Networks

Unblock
Encourages voluntary, meaningful engagement instead of forced participation.
Problem it addresses
Forcing participation leads to disengagement and resentment, reducing the quality of involvement.
The move
Create engagement processes that invite voluntary, meaningful contributions rather than mandating participation.
Related patterns

Problem it addresses

The move
Develop "edge spaces" where outside voices can interact with core processes, bringing fresh insights while maintaining group integrity.
Related patterns
Invite, Don't ObligePermeable Boundaries

Problem it addresses

The move

School of Systems

This pattern language was developed by the School of Systems and Complexity (SSC) over 8 years of practitioner work across philanthropic networks, peacebuilding ecosystems, and social change organisations. Patterns are not prescriptions — they are names for things that are already happening. The value of naming them is that you can then choose.
